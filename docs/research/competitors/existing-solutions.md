# Research: Existing Solutions
**Question:** What apps already exist for PTSD self-monitoring, and why do they succeed or fail?

---

## The Landscape

The most established PTSD apps come from the VA (US Department of Veterans Affairs) — developed with clinical oversight, extensively researched, and free.

---

## PTSD Coach (VA) — The Main Competitor

Developed by the VA National Center for PTSD. Free, widely used, backed by clinical research.

**What it does:**
- Education about PTSD symptoms
- Self-assessment using the PCL (PTSD Checklist) — a validated clinical measure
- Access to professional care resources
- Coping tools for managing daily stress
- Support network access

**Version 4 (2024) added:**
- Personalized home screen with activity recommendations
- Trigger tracker
- Mood tracker
- Medication tracker
- Progress tracking over time

**Research outcome:** PTSD Coach participants experienced significantly greater reductions in PTSD symptoms and depression than controls. Clinically significant decreases were higher in the PTSD Coach group.

**Why it matters for Trigger Log:** PTSD Coach v4 now includes a trigger tracker. The space is no longer empty. Trigger Log must differentiate by being *radically simpler and faster* — not by offering a feature that PTSD Coach doesn't have.

---

## PE Coach — Therapy-Integrated Tool

Designed to be used *during* Prolonged Exposure therapy with a trained therapist. Not a standalone tool.

**Key characteristic:** Requires professional supervision. Not for self-help.

**Why it matters:** Shows the clinical community acknowledges that apps work best when integrated with therapy — not as replacement.

---

## CBT-i Coach — Sleep-Focused

Developed in partnership with Stanford University. Focused on insomnia related to PTSD. Not directly relevant to trigger tracking.

---

## PTSD Family Coach

For family members of people with PTSD. Education and support for caregivers. Not directly relevant.

---

## Why Existing Tools Are Complex

All VA apps share the same structural problem: they are multi-purpose. They have menus, categories, educational content, coping exercises, assessments. They require navigation. They require cognitive capacity.

PTSD Coach may have added a trigger tracker in 2024, but it lives several taps deep inside a complex app. During or after a trigger event, navigating to that feature is not realistic.

**This is Trigger Log's actual competitive advantage:** not features, but access speed. One tap from the home screen or widget, no navigation, done.

---

## What Is Missing in the Current Market

| Gap | Trigger Log's response |
|---|---|
| Existing apps require navigation during crisis | Single-tap from widget / lock screen |
| Trigger logging buried inside feature-heavy apps | Logging is the entire app |
| Pattern views designed for wellness (not trauma safety) | Needs a safe review design |
| No easy therapist export | Should be a first-class feature |
| Apps built for veterans (English, US-centric) | Designed for Israeli civilian population |
