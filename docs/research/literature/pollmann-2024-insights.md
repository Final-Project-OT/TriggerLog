# Insights — Pollmann et al. (2024)

**Full citation:**
Pollmann Y, Clancy KJ, Devignes Q, Ren B, Kaufman M, Rosso IM (2024).
*Ecological Momentary Assessments of Trauma-Related Intrusive Memories: Potential Clinical Utility.*
McLean Hospital / Harvard Medical School. medRxiv preprint, May 2024.

---

## What They Did

131 participants with trauma exposure completed daily smartphone-based EMA surveys for two weeks — 5 short check-ins per day. No treatment was given. Just documentation.

They measured changes across all 4 PTSD symptom clusters (DSM-5):
- Cluster B: Intrusion (flashbacks, intrusive memories)
- Cluster C: Avoidance
- Cluster D: Negative cognitions/mood
- Cluster E: Hyperarousal

---

## Key Findings

- **Intrusion symptoms (Cluster B) dropped significantly** over the two weeks — p=0.006
- The effect was **specific to intrusive memories** — the other 3 clusters did not change
- The improvement was **independent of** initial severity, treatment status, and trauma type
- In other words: simply noticing and logging intrusions — repeatedly, in real time — changed something

---

## Why This Matters for TriggerLog

This is the core scientific justification for the app's existence:

> Logging a trigger — without analysis, without therapy, without explanation — may itself be therapeutic.

The mechanism is not fully understood, but the leading hypothesis is **metacognitive distancing**: the act of observing and naming an intrusion creates a small separation between the person and the experience. "It happened. I noted it. I moved on."

TriggerLog is built entirely on this principle:
- No advice
- No coping instructions
- No explanations required
- Just: mark that it happened

---

## Honest Limitations

| Limitation | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| No control group | Can't rule out natural symptom decay over time |
| Pre-print only | Has not passed full peer review as of June 2026 |
| 5 check-ins/day | Far more intensive than TriggerLog's single tap — we don't know if lower frequency produces the same effect |
| Self-selected sample | Participants motivated to engage with trauma research may not represent the broader PTSD population |

## What We Said About It in the Presentation (Slide 4)

- Research question: Does real-time documentation reduce intrusion symptoms without any treatment?
- Method: 131 participants, 2 weeks, smartphone only
- Result: Intrusion cluster improved significantly (p=0.006)
- Critique: No control group; pre-print; protocol more intensive than TriggerLog
- Connection: Justifies minimal-interaction, real-time logging as a design model
