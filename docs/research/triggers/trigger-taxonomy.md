# Research: Trigger Taxonomy
**Question:** What are the clinically recognized trigger types in PTSD, and can users accurately identify them?

---

## DSM-5 Classification

The DSM-5 does not enumerate a fixed list of trigger "types." Instead, it classifies triggers by their origin:

**External triggers** — stimuli in the environment:
- Sounds, smells, visual scenes
- Specific places, people, conversations
- Activities that resemble the trauma context
- Dates, anniversaries, times of day

**Internal triggers** — stimuli from within the body and mind:
- Distressing memories or intrusive thoughts
- Body sensations and visceral feelings
- Emotional states (fear, shame, guilt, anger)
- Physical sensations associated with the original trauma

Research also identifies a **sensory processing dimension**: how stimuli from the external world (touch, sound, visual) and the internal world (visceral, physical) are processed differently in traumatized individuals. PTSD involves altered sensitivity in both channels simultaneously.

---

## Can Users Accurately Identify Their Own Triggers?

**The critical finding:** A 2013 study asked 46 trauma survivors to complete a daily diary identifying possible intrusions. Over 7 consecutive days, participants reported 294 intrusions total. The researchers found that **participants were significantly unaware of the triggers that led to their intrusive memories**.

This has direct implications for any app asking users to select a trigger type:
- The user may genuinely not know what triggered them
- The identified type may be the *most salient* stimulus, not the *actual* trigger
- Real-time logging is more accurate than retrospective recall — but still imperfect

**Practical implication for app design:** Trigger type selection should be:
1. Optional, not required
2. A short list (5–7 options max) without pressure to be precise
3. Treated as approximate signal, not clinical diagnosis

---

## Practical Trigger Categories (Clinical + Therapeutic Sources)

Based on therapy worksheets and clinical practice (not DSM-5 directly), common working categories are:

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| Sound | Sudden loud noise, voices, music |
| Visual | Faces, scenes, images |
| Smell | Specific scents tied to trauma |
| Crowd / space | Enclosed spaces, large groups, public transport |
| Memory / thought | Intrusive recall, anniversary |
| Body sensation | Heart racing, touch, physical pain |
| Emotional state | Feeling helpless, trapped, ashamed |
| Other / unknown | Cannot identify |

The "unknown" category is clinically significant — research shows it will be selected often and should not be hidden or de-emphasized.

---

## Key Takeaway

Trigger taxonomy in the app is a **best-effort approximation**, not a diagnostic tool. The value of logging comes from the timestamp and pattern over time — not from the accuracy of any single type label.
