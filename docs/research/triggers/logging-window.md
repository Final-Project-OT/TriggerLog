# Research: The Logging Window
**Question:** Is the concept of logging "after the moment passes" realistic? What is the actual cognitive state post-trigger?

---

## What Happens Cognitively After a Trigger

When a PTSD trigger activates the threat response, several cognitive systems are impaired:

- **Working memory** — reduced capacity to hold and process information
- **Attention** — narrowed, difficulty shifting focus
- **Time perception** — distorted; PTSD patients show significant time overestimation compared to controls
- **Executive function** — planning, decision-making, sequencing all degraded

These impairments do not stop the moment the acute response ends. They persist through the recovery window.

---

## How Long Does Hyperarousal Last?

There is no fixed answer — and this is clinically significant.

- Some individuals recover to baseline within **20–60 minutes**
- Others experience elevated arousal for **several hours**
- In severe cases, hyperarousal can persist for **days**

Duration depends on: trigger intensity, current stress load, therapy stage, coping capacity, and individual neurobiology.

**The app's concept assumes a short, predictable window.** Research says this window is real but highly variable in length and depth.

---

## The Ecological Momentary Assessment (EMA) Validation

The app's core methodology — logging a brief signal in the moment rather than journaling retrospectively — maps directly to **Ecological Momentary Assessment (EMA)**, a validated research technique.

EMA studies on PTSD have confirmed:
- Real-time data collection is more accurate than end-of-day or weekly recall
- Brief, low-burden check-ins have significantly higher compliance than structured journaling
- EMA captures patterns that retrospective self-report consistently misses

**This is strong validation.** The single-tap approach is not just intuitive — it is methodologically aligned with the best-practice research methodology for this population.

---

## Compliance: Will People Actually Log?

The evidence is mixed and nuanced:

**In favor of compliance:**
- Brief, low-burden interactions (seconds, not minutes) have higher completion rates
- EMA studies demonstrate sustained compliance over weeks when burden is low
- People with PTSD are often highly motivated to understand their own patterns

**Against compliance:**
- Journaling is explicitly not recommended for everyone, especially in early trauma recovery
- Some individuals find any engagement with their trauma — even a single tap — re-activating
- The post-trigger window is the hardest moment to form new behaviors
- App fatigue is a documented problem in mental health tools

**Key implication:** The app must not require opening it during the trigger. It must have a pathway (widget, lock screen shortcut) that is accessible without navigation, and logging must be possible even when motor control and decision-making are degraded.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. The logging window is real but not reliably short — design must work across a range of cognitive states
2. EMA is the clinical gold standard for exactly this kind of logging — the app has research backing
3. Compliance is achievable but not guaranteed — friction must be near zero
4. Some users will never be able to log during or after triggers — the app should not assume universal access to this window
