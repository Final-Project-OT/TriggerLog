# Trigger Log — App Concept
**Course:** UI/UX Design  
**Assignment:** Final Project  
**Date:** 2026-05-25

---

## The Problem

People living with PTSD experience unexpected triggers throughout their daily life — a sudden loud noise, a familiar smell, a crowded space, a specific time of day. In the moment, the brain enters survival mode and there is no room for analysis or reflection.

The problem is not the trigger itself. The problem is the **invisibility of patterns**.

Without knowing *when*, *where*, and *what type* of triggers occur most often, therapy moves slowly. The person cannot explain what's happening to them clearly. The therapist works with incomplete information. Progress is harder to measure.

Yet during a trigger, opening an app and writing about it is impossible — the cognitive and emotional load is too high.

---

## The Insight

There is a window — **after the moment passes** — when the person has just enough capacity to do one small thing. Not write a paragraph. Not describe their feelings. Just mark: *"that happened."*

That single tap, repeated over time, becomes a map.

---

## The Solution

**Trigger Log** is a single-purpose app for people with PTSD.

One action: **log that a trigger just happened.**

The app captures:
- Timestamp (automatic)
- Location (automatic, optional)
- Trigger type (one tap from a small set: sound, crowd, smell, memory, visual, other)

Nothing else is required. The user is done in under 5 seconds.

Over time, the app surfaces patterns:
- What times of day are hardest
- Which locations feel least safe
- What trigger types appear most

This data belongs to the user. They can share it with their therapist as a simple visual summary — a tool for better, faster, more honest therapy conversations.

---

## What This App Does NOT Do

- It does not try to calm the user during a crisis
- It does not give advice or instructions
- It does not require writing, typing, or explaining
- It does not connect to any government system or external service

These boundaries are intentional. Apps that try to intervene during a PTSD crisis require cognitive capacity the user does not have. Trigger Log works *around* that reality, not against it.

---

## Who It's For

- Adults living with PTSD (any cause — combat, trauma, accidents, violence)
- Primary context: Israel, where PTSD is prevalent and underserved by accessible tools
- Secondary: globally applicable, no language dependency in the core interaction

---

## Core UX Principles

| Principle | Why it matters for PTSD |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| **Under 5 seconds to log** | Cognitive load is still elevated post-trigger |
| **No required text input** | Writing requires executive function that may still be impaired |
| **Calm, non-alarming visual design** | Avoid re-activating the stress response |
| **No notifications by default** | Notifications themselves can be triggers |
| **Full user control of data** | Trust and safety are non-negotiable for this population |

---

## The Single User Flow

```
User feels a trigger → moment passes → opens app → taps "Log" → 
optionally taps trigger type → done.
```

That's it.

---

## Value Over Time

| After... | What the user gains |
|----------|---------------------|
| 1 week | Awareness that triggers are happening more/less than felt |
| 1 month | Clear pattern of times, places, types |
| 3 months | Concrete data to bring to therapy |
| 6 months | Measurable before/after comparison as treatment progresses |

---

## Why This Works as a Single-Purpose App

Shazam does one thing: identify a song. You don't go to Shazam to discover music, make playlists, or read lyrics. You go because you need to know *what song this is*, right now, fast.

Trigger Log does one thing: mark that a trigger happened. Everything else — the patterns, the insights, the therapy value — emerges naturally from that single repeated action.

The simplicity is not a limitation. It is the design.
